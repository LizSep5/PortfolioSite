SharkNinja Fan Sensor

ENGN 0620 Design Brief | Brown University


Completed:

May 2025

Timeline:

3 Months (10 hrs/week)

Role:

Design, Electronics

To aid in the testing of their new fan products, our team created a fan testing device for the household product company SharkNinja.




Problem Definition and Research

Our team met with a client from SharkNinja to identify goals for our device. Our device would replace the lab's existing solutions which are large, difficult to move, and expensive, requiring 36 individual anemometers. After conducting a literature review of existing air flow characterization systems, we concluded that we needed to create an array for data collection at many discrete points, and a base to move the sensor around the array.


Design Requirements

  • Data to Measure: Volumetric Air Flow, Temperature
  • Size Specifications: 2x2ft Measurement Field from 6 feet away from user
  • Qualities: Compact, Easy to Move, Reproducible at a Reasonable Cost




    • Ideation and Fabrication

    Our array brainstorm led us to two possible options, an X-Y Grid or Radial Grid. We made preliminary prototypes of each and landed on an X-Y Grid due to concerns with the torque and imbalance of the Radial Grid.

    For our base, we tested several options, including a triangular truss and "whiteboard" style legs, choosing the whiteboard style due to feasibility.

    After constructing a prototype out of scrap wood, we conducted a Vibration Test and realized that the base swayed significantly due to unrestricted axes of movement on the joints. After constructing a second prototype out of stronger, scrap metal, we confirmed that this was a structural and not material problem. To combat this, we made each joint a truss by adding supports connecting the legs to the feet and top of the structure.




    To construct the final version, we used aluminum 20/20 extrusions and two OpenBuilds linear actuators, all readily availible and highly adaptable components that would ensure easy reproduction. We cut the pieces down on the BDW's bandsaw and table saw and assembled them with a variety of connecting brackets.



    To collect volumetric air flow data, our design employs a digital anemometer (Modern Device Rev C anemometer), which we calibrated in the Brown Design Workshop's wind tunnel. It also features a DHT11 Humidity Sensor to determine the temperature, humidity, and relative humidity of SharkNinja fans, some of which having a mist feature.



    To house our electronics, I designed a removable, multi-stage finger-jointed box which we fabricated using lasered acrylic and glue. The final version features a velcro strap for easy removability.
